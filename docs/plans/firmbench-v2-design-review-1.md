VERDICT: NEEDS_REVISION

## Summary Assessment

The "matched quad" framing and the keystone-first sequencing are sound, and the verifier's proportional-holdout trick actually survives the LTV change (verified). But the design has three critical, code-verifiable defects: Phase A **bundles five latents that produce the same observable and move together**, collapsing v1's clean one-unknown-per-experiment identifiability; the experiment space blows up to ~100x the $6k/H=10 budget with no compensating increase in budget or horizon; and the regret-vs-oracle ceiling becomes invalid because the proposed greedy oracle is no longer a provable upper bound (v1's "oracle" is already only a heuristic). Phase C is also mis-scoped: the "first LLM in the stack" already exists in `scorer.py`.

## Critical Issues (must fix)

### C1. Phase A destroys identifiability: five latents collapse onto one observable
This is the #1 risk and the design under-rates it. v1 is learnable for a specific structural reason the design does not name: **only one unknown moves per experiment, and the demand signal is free.** Concretely:

- The scripted experimenter discovers demand from `audience` (the matching-pool size), which is returned independent of spend/impressions. A $10 probe with only 2 impressions still reports the full pool size. Verified: `sim.py:241-243` returns `audience = len(pool)`; a $10 probe on seed 1 reports `audience=1051` while reaching only 2 users. So **popularity discovery is effectively free**, and the only thing that costs impressions is feature-attribution (`purchases > 0.5`).
- Feature attribution is clean because the protocol holds price fixed (known/controlled) and builds exactly one feature, then re-probes — the *delta* in purchases attributes that feature to that pain (`sim.py:404-414`, `ScriptedExperimenter`). One unknown moves; the bijection guarantees the delta is all-or-nothing.

Phase A adds **segment weights, channel↔segment mapping, per-user elasticity, quality_bar, and segment-varied churn** — and crucially, several of these produce the *same* observable and move *together* with the build:
- "tries > 0, purchases ≈ 0" is now produced by (a) feature unbuilt, (b) price too high, (c) `ff < quality_bar` (the hard gate in the design's buy formula), and (e) low-elasticity segment. I verified all three collapse to the same observation: with one build+probe you cannot tell unbuilt-feature from below-quality-bar from wrong-segment. (Demonstrated against the live funnel: cases a/b/c all yield `tries≈18, purchases≈0–1`.)
- "tries ≈ 0" is now produced by both wrong-pain-target *and* wrong-channel (`channel_fit≈0.25`), a different axis the agent must also probe.

Why it breaks the design: the matched-quad table (design L130-136) lists a clean observation per latent ("tries-but-no-buys when ff<bar", "try/audience varies by channel"), but these observations are **not orthogonal** — the same campaign result is consistent with multiple latent states. Discovery degrades from "run the protocol" to "solve a confounded inverse problem," i.e. luck, which is precisely the Vending-Bench failure the PLAN warns against (PLAN.md L226). The design's only mitigation is "validate the scripted experimenter after each phase" (design L254) — but it does not show that a scripted experimenter *can* be written that disentangles these within budget, and the gate as written (design L137-142) is hand-waved ("Update ScriptedExperimenter to also discover segments/channels").

Suggested fix: make the legs **separately observable**, not just collectively present. Concretely: (1) report a per-channel audience/try breakdown so channel and pain are probeable on different axes (the design hints at this at L96 but the buy-side confound remains); (2) give quality_bar a *distinct* signal from price — e.g. expose a "tried but bounced at checkout" count vs "tried but didn't convert on price," so the gate is identifiable separately from elasticity; (3) **before locking Phase A, write the disentangling ScriptedExperimenter and show it clears naive within budget** — that is the actual gate, and it must be a deliverable, not a TODO. If a clean experimenter cannot be written, the latent must be cut or made cheaper to observe.

### C2. Experiment-space blowup vs an unchanged $6k / H=10 budget
The design raises pools to 16-20, adds K channels, K segments, a price grid, and graded many-to-many `fit(p,f)`, but explicitly keeps "H=10 and $6k... unchanged" is never reconsidered (the design never mentions scaling either). The budget math does not close:

- v1: round-0 probe of 8 pains = $80; the bijection means each build attributes to one pain, and builds cost 8×$300=$2400 of $6000. It already fits only because attribution is all-or-nothing and demand is free.
- Phase B many-to-many `fit(p,f) ∈ [0,1]`: a build now partially lifts *many* pains, so `purchases > 0.5` no longer attributes a feature to one pain. To estimate a fractional `fit` you must vary f while holding p and collect enough impressions to beat the per-cell signal — on a 16×16 grid that is 256 cells × ~$60 ≈ $15,360, already 2.5x the entire budget.
- Phase A+B combined (pain × feature × channel × price) is 16×16×3×11 = 8,448 cells; even at $60/cell to estimate a *fractional* effect that is ~$500k vs $6k. (price_grid is 11 wide, verified `sim.py:37`.)

Why it breaks the design: the learnability constraint requires "a disciplined experimenter must discover each latent within H=10 and $6,000." With the space this large and the budget fixed, the scripted experimenter cannot identify the structure; it will fall back to guessing, `naive < scripted` will narrow or invert, and the gate fails. The design acknowledges "identifiability explosion" in the risk table (L254) but the mitigation ("matched quad") does not address *budget*, only *presence of an observation*.

Suggested fix: make horizon and cash **explicit difficulty dials that scale with pool/channel/segment count** (the design already proposes a `Config`-driven curriculum at L154-157 — extend it to H and cash). State a target identifiability budget (e.g. "the easy rung must be solvable in ≤ N probe-dollars by the reference experimenter") and tune dials to it per curriculum rung. Strongly prefer the **sparse** fit option (design [OPEN B1], L162) and keep segment/channel counts small (K≈3) at the easy rungs.

### C3. Regret-vs-oracle becomes an invalid ceiling once the oracle is greedy
The design defers to a "greedy/approximate oracle" for dense fit + segments (L160-164, [OPEN B2]) and asserts "regret stays meaningful." That assertion is unsupported and likely false. Two verified facts make this critical:

- v1's `OraclePolicy` is **already not optimal** — it is a fixed heuristic: top-6 pains, build all of them, `best_price_for` with a 600-user sample (`sim.py:348-380`). It only *looks* like a ceiling because the world is simple enough that the heuristic is near-optimal and the scripted experimenter pays an information cost the oracle doesn't (54% disc.eff, scripted beats oracle in 0/30 seeds — verified).
- Once the world has many-to-many fit + segments + retention, a greedy oracle makes locally-greedy build/channel/price choices that a smarter *learned* agent (with full-episode lookahead and LTV reasoning) can exceed. The moment `scripted > oracle` or `RL > oracle`, "regret vs oracle" goes negative and the metric is meaningless as a ceiling.

This is not hypothetical: the v1 verifier eval already shows the oracle getting **flagged 6/21** by the cheat tripwire (PLAN.md L34, run.py output) because its self-reported profit diverges from held-out — i.e. the "oracle" is not even internally consistent with the grader. Scaling that shaky oracle into a harder world compounds the problem.

Why it breaks the design: the stated Phase D gate is literally "regret vs single-agent oracle" (design L242), and the primary diagnostic across the whole bench is "regret vs oracle" (PLAN.md L83). If the oracle isn't a valid upper bound, the headline metric is uninterpretable.

Suggested fix: either (a) keep the world tractable enough (sparse fit, small K) that an **exact or provably-near-optimal** oracle is computable, and prove the bound; or (b) drop "regret vs oracle" as a *ceiling* metric and rename it — report it as "gap vs a strong reference policy," and add a guard that *fails loudly* if any policy beats the reference (signaling the reference must be strengthened). Do not call a greedy heuristic an "oracle."

### C4. Phase C mis-scoped: the LLM judge already exists; the novel/risky part is the *reward-folded* evaluator, and its honesty tripwire is not computable as specified
The design states Phase C is "The first LLM in the stack" (design L168) and frames the translator (copy→craft, spec→quality) as new work. **This is factually wrong against the code.** `scorer.py` already implements `score_ad_copy` → `{craft, target_pains}` and `score_feature_spec` → `{feature_id, quality}` via a cheap LLM (`scorer.py:125-161`), wired into `env.py:39,208-209,256-257` and exposed through the MCP tools' optional `ad_copy`/`spec` params (`env.py:192,241`), gated behind `fast_mode`. So Job 1 (translator) is built. This matters because it means Phase C's real, unbuilt, and risky content is **Job 2: folding a process/strategy score into reward** (design L190-214) — and that piece has a concrete defect:

- The "judge-honesty tripwire" requires the quality score to "predict held-out execution outcomes ... high quality + ≈0 held-out conversion lift → flag" (design L210-213). But **"conversion lift attributable to a specific artifact" is not cleanly computable** in this sim. Per round the agent changes build, price, channel, target, *and* copy simultaneously; the funnel multiplies craft × resonance × p_buy (`env.py:104-113`). To attribute lift to the artifact alone you need a counterfactual (same round, same everything, craft=1.0) — which the verifier does not run and the design does not specify. Without a clean counterfactual, the tripwire correlates quality against a quantity dominated by build/price/targeting noise, so it will either never fire (useless) or fire on honest agents (harmful).

Why it breaks the design: the entire anti-reward-hacking argument for the minority-weight evaluator rests on this tripwire tying `w_q` back to ground truth (design L208-213). If the attribution is not computable, the guardrail is decorative and the `w_q·quality_norm` term is a free, gameable reward channel — exactly the failure mode the PLAN's "judge → params, never reward" principle was designed to avoid (PLAN.md referenced at design L168).

Suggested fix: (1) correct the design to state the translator exists and scope Phase C as "activate translator out of fast_mode + add the reward-folded evaluator." (2) Make attribution computable by having the verifier run a **paired counterfactual replay** (artifact-on vs artifact-off, same seed/actions) so "held-out lift attributable to the artifact" is an actual measured delta — this reuses the existing replay machinery. (3) Given the cost/complexity, seriously consider **cutting Job 2 entirely** (keep the process-judge as a reported diagnostic only, as v1 PLAN already intended and as the design itself lists as the safer "Optional diagnostic" at L184) — the core thesis (RL-native experimental discovery) does not need reward-folded process scoring, and `w_p ≫ w_q` already concedes it is marginal.

### C5. LLM-judge determinism is not achievable under RL exploration; this silently breaks the "deterministic env" property
The design's determinism story is "cache keyed on `(artifact_text, segment)`" (design L180, L256). This gives determinism only for **byte-identical** text. RL training and even temperature>0 eval generate novel artifact text on essentially every rollout, so the cache hit rate is ~0 during the exact workload that needs reproducibility, and you inherit: nondeterministic reward, latency, and per-rollout API cost. Verified aggravating factor: the existing scorer calls the model at `temperature=0.1`, not 0 (`scorer.py:113`), and parses the first number from free text (`scorer.py:91-99`) — so even identical text is not guaranteed identical, and near-identical text is definitely not.

Why it breaks the design: constraint (2) is "the sim stays DETERMINISTIC/reproducible." An LLM in the reward path with a content-addressed cache satisfies this only for replay of *logged* episodes, not for training. The design claims reproducible training (design "deterministic env" property; risk table L256) but the mechanism doesn't deliver it for novel text.

Suggested fix: be explicit that the LLM judge makes training **non-deterministic by construction**, and confine determinism claims to *graded replay of a fixed action+artifact log* (which the cache does support). For training reproducibility, either (a) set judge `temperature=0` and pin a model snapshot and accept residual provider nondeterminism, or (b) keep the LLM out of the reward path during RL — use the rule-based targeting extraction (already in `scorer.py:39-63`, zero-cost, deterministic) for the funnel and reserve the LLM judge for *eval/leaderboard* only. Option (b) preserves the deterministic-env thesis cleanly.

## Suggestions (nice to have)

- **Doc/code drift on the funnel constant.** Both PLAN.md (L138, L249) and the design (v1 inventory L47) state `impressions = spend × 0.1` / `impressions_per_dollar=0.1`, but the code is `0.2` (`sim.py:30`, verified). Since the design reasons about budget/reach feasibility, fix the constant in the docs or the analysis is off by 2x.
- **Stale learnability numbers.** PLAN.md L21 claims "naive loses money (−2.1k)"; the current `sim.py` prints naive **+4035** mean (verified). The design inherits the "naive loses money" premise (gate `naive < scripted < oracle` is about ordering, which still holds, but the magnitude claim is wrong and the naive floor being *positive* slightly weakens the "discovery is binding" story). Re-baseline before using these as a gate.
- **`probe_market ≡ run_campaign` is literally true in code** (`env.py:307-317` — run_campaign just calls probe_market). The design lists this under v1 "currently trivial" (L39) — fine — but Phase A's "cheap probe vs expensive exploit" identifiability relies on them being *behaviorally* different (small vs large spend). With free `audience`, spend size barely matters for demand discovery; make sure the channel/segment probes have a real marginal cost or the explore/exploit tradeoff stays vestigial.
- **Quality dimension vs build budget tension.** Phase A turns on `implementation_quality` and adds `quality_bar` gates, but building is 1/round at $300 (`sim.py:273-276`) and there is no separate "invest more in quality" lever — quality comes only from the (deferred) Phase C spec judge. So in Phase A the "invest in quality / build" action in the matched quad (L135) has **no actual lever** until Phase C lands, making that quad's action-leg missing for a whole phase. Either add a numeric quality-investment action in Phase A or move the quality_bar latent to Phase C where its lever exists.
- **Multi-agent (Phase D) coordination-tax metric inherits C3.** "Coordination tax = regret vs single-agent full-info oracle" (design L226-228) is only meaningful if that oracle is a valid ceiling — same defect as C3, compounded by partial observability. Gate D explicitly on C3 being resolved.
- **YAGNI / value-per-effort.** Best value/effort is clearly **Phase A** (the keystone — genuinely new, and LTV is the differentiator) and **activating the already-built translator** (Phase C Job 1, near-zero effort). Phase B is cheap dials *but* is where the identifiability/oracle problems bite hardest — do it conservatively (sparse, small K). Phase C Job 2 (reward-folded evaluator) and Phase D are the highest-risk/lowest-marginal-thesis-value; recommend cutting Job 2 and treating D as a research stretch, consistent with the PLAN's own framing (PLAN.md L67, "stretch").

## Verified Claims (confirmed correct against the code)

- **The verifier's proportional-holdout scaling survives the LTV/subscription change** — *contingent on the sim staying expectation-based.* I walked a 2-round subscription example: because `base`, `recurring`, and `new_subscribers` are all linear in population scale and the sim never rounds subscribers to integers, the compounding base stays linear, so `reported_full × holdout_frac == holdout_profit` (gap 0.0). This holds even with **responsive** churn, because `price_pressure`/`quality_gap` depend on price/quality (not on scale), so `churn_eff` is identical at full and holdout scale. The gap-tripwire calibration (15%, `run.py:40,125-127`) is therefore *not* broken by compounding per se. **The one thing that WOULD break it** (and the design must forbid): any term that depends on the *absolute* subscriber count — network effects, capacity caps, market saturation, or integer-rounding subscribers. The design's Phase A does not introduce such a term, so it is safe today; add an explicit invariant ("all population effects must be linear/expectation-based — no absolute-count nonlinearities") so a future phase doesn't silently break the grader.
- **The matched-quad design principle is sound** and correctly diagnoses the failure modes (no-observation → unlearnable, etc.). The v1 entity/system inventory (design L34-51) is accurate against `sim.py`/`run.py`.
- **Keystone sequencing is real, not cosmetic** — A (persona+LTV) genuinely is the structural change the others layer on; B is a re-parameterization of the same `Config`; the NL layer genuinely attaches at the action artifacts (`env.py` tool params already exist). No big-bang rework is *forced* by the layering itself (the rework risk is in C1/C2/C3, not in the phase boundaries).
- **Determinism of the deterministic core** — `generate_world` is seeded and the funnel sums probabilities rather than sampling (`sim.py:233-259`), so the non-LLM sim is reproducible as claimed. Names/keywords are generated *after* demography to preserve the RNG stream (`sim.py:174-178`) — a real, correct precaution.
- **Game-resistance of the v1 grader** — secret 20% holdout + full replay + gap tripwire is implemented as described (`run.py:45-138`, `env.py:64-130`); it does re-compute profit rather than trust reported numbers.
